Known Limitations (be honest about these)

1. Resolution — highest priority

Dashcam footage is 640×360 pixels. A plastic bottle occupies 3–5 pixels. In 3 of 39 validated R-75 frames, even the human labeler could not distinguish snow from litter on a full-screen monitor.

Mitigation
  • Conservative prompt: "When in doubt about small items, score 1 and mark uncertain=true."
  • R-21 tested at 960×540 showed noticeably cleaner detection.
Recommendation

Production deployment should use higher-resolution dashcams. This single change would have the largest impact on accuracy.

2. KAB methodology mismatch

KAB was designed for trained human inspectors sitting in a van at full visual resolution, scoring overall impression at driving speed. Our AI sees one forward-facing frame at 640×360. Even KAB uses 3–6 inspectors and averages because borderline cases are inherently ambiguous. We align well on clearly clean vs clearly dirty, but 1-vs-2 boundaries have ±1 variance.

Mitigation

The uncertain field flags borderline cases for human review.

3. Counting is unreliable

GPT-4o cannot reliably count individual small objects. Our scoring uses visual impression matching KAB's text definitions — which mirrors how human KAB inspectors actually work.

4. Facility false positives

Frames 47 and 48 on R-75 are the city's garbage depot. AI correctly detected clutter, but no enforcement should occur there. Production needs a facility exclusion list from the city.

5. Curbside collection context

On collection days, residents legitimately place trash bins and bags at the curb. Current prompt explicitly treats curbside bins as NOT a violation. Collection-day timing integration is a documented future-work item.

6. GPS availability on new footage

The April 16 videos don't have synchronized GPS. Bulk item detection works (image-only), but address matching requires Samsara's dual video+GPS export path.

7. Owner data privacy

City legal restrictions prevent sharing property owner data. Warning letters use placeholder names; "shotgun approach" matches top 5 nearest properties. In production, the city plugs its owner database into the pipeline — the system never sees it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How did you get from your previous scoring to KAB 1-4?
Our previous output used confidence tiers like LOW/MEDIUM/HIGH. We rewrote the prompt to directly output KAB 1-4 integers on each of the five indices, with evidence and rule fields attached to every score. The Calibration Report shows the match against human labels.
Does the system detect every bulk item?
It detects the ones clearly visible. In 90 frames of April 16 footage it caught 10 frames with bulk items. At 640×360, items partially obscured or at the extreme edge of frame can be missed. Frame 42 in the R-75 route is an example — a small cardboard box partially hidden behind a parked car that even the human labeler had to search to see.
Can the system count items?
No. GPT-4o has known limitations counting small individual objects. Instead, we use visual impression scoring matching KAB's text definitions. This matches how KAB human inspectors actually work — they score impression at driving speed, not by counting.
What about false positives?
Out of 35 decidable frames in the calibration set, AI produced two moderate-level false positives, both at score 2 (not severe). Frame 0041 misread dried liquid residue and building wall shadow as graffiti. Frame 0023 misread snow patches as graffiti. Both are at the 640×360 resolution floor — higher resolution would eliminate this class of error.
When can this go into production?
The pipeline runs today. For production deployment, the city needs three things: an OpenAI API account, a facility exclusion list, and synchronized GPS+video exports from Samsara. The 06_SOURCE_CODE/ folder is ready to hand off.
How much does it cost?
$0.15–0.22 per 10-minute video. Pilot of 5 trucks/week: $30–50. Full city with 10 trucks weekly: $200–350. Monthly: $800–1,400.
What about real-time detection?
Not in scope for this capstone. The current system processes recorded footage, which is cheaper and more reliable than real-time. Real-time is documented as a recommended future enhancement.
Can you show us a specific warning letter?
Yes — they're on the Warning Letters page. We use a placeholder owner name because of the city's legal restriction on sharing owner data. In production, the city plugs in its property owner database — we never see that data.
How do we know the AI is actually right and not hallucinating?
The Calibration Report is the answer. 35 frames, human-scored independently, compared to AI output: 86% exact match, 0 disagreements of ≥2 points. Every AI score is accompanied by an evidence field listing what it saw, which you can verify against the image on the Evidence Cards page.
The previous version had confidence levels like HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW. What happened to that?
Removed. Those tiers were comparing a confidence label to a human category rather than a real calibration. Everything is now KAB 1-4 on both the human and AI side, which is a like-for-like comparison.
Why did you use sofas as an example and not mattresses or refrigerators?
Because that's what was in the city-provided footage. We detected whatever was there. The prompt lists all bulk item types (sofa, mattress, refrigerator, TV, tire, construction debris, large appliance, furniture). If a mattress appears in future footage, it will be detected by name.
